## What Downtown Miami Condo Sellers Face in 2026

[Selling a condo in Downtown Miami](/sell-downtown-miami) in 2026 is a more documentation-intensive process than it was three years ago. Florida's structural integrity legislation — Senate Bill 4-D — introduced mandatory milestone inspection and reserve requirements that have materially changed how buyers, buyer's agents, and lenders approach condo acquisitions in buildings three stories and taller.

For Downtown Miami sellers, this is not an abstract regulatory development. It is the first conversation buyers and their advisors initiate. Before discussing price, condition, or view, a sophisticated Downtown Miami buyer or their agent will ask: what is the building's milestone inspection status, what are the reserves, and are there pending special assessments?

The seller who has answers to those questions prepared before listing is in a fundamentally different position than one who learns about the building's financial situation during the due diligence period.

## The Building-Level Reality

Downtown Miami's residential market is almost entirely condominium. The overwhelming majority of properties available to buyers in the urban core are units within high-rise or mid-rise condominium associations — not fee-simple residential. That means the asset being transacted is not just the unit itself, but the unit's relationship to the building and association that governs it.

Buyers in this market — particularly investor buyers and international buyers who are approaching the acquisition analytically — are purchasing into a building's financial condition as much as they are purchasing a specific unit. A well-appointed unit in a building with deferred inspections and insufficient reserves is a less competitive asset than a comparable unit in a building with clean documentation and a funded reserve.

### What Florida's SB 4-D Requires

The milestone inspection requirement applies to buildings three stories or taller. Buildings reaching 30 years of age (or 25 years if within three miles of the coastline) must complete a Phase 1 milestone inspection conducted by a licensed architect or engineer. If the Phase 1 inspection identifies substantial structural deterioration, a Phase 2 inspection is triggered.

The reserve funding requirement mandates that associations fund reserves for specific components — roof, load-bearing walls, floor/ceiling assemblies, and other structural elements — based on the building's remaining useful life. Associations that previously maintained minimal reserves are now required to rebuild them, often through special assessments.

For sellers, the relevant questions are:
- Has the building completed its required milestone inspection?
- What did that inspection find?
- Is the reserve fund currently meeting the new statutory minimums?
- Are any special assessments pending or recently passed?

The answers to these questions will surface during buyer due diligence regardless of what a seller discloses. Knowing them in advance — and being able to present the building's documentation proactively — positions the transaction differently.

## Who Buys in Downtown Miami

Downtown Miami's buyer composition differs meaningfully from the family-oriented and move-up markets that characterize most of South Florida. The active buyer pool in the urban core leans heavily toward:

> **Investor buyers** targeting rental yield. Downtown Miami's proximity to Brickell, Port Miami, and the Brightline rail hub supports a professional and transient rental tenant base. Buyers pursuing income-producing investment analyze net operating income, building rental restrictions, and the depth of the existing rental market before committing.

> **International buyers** from Latin America and Europe who are acquiring an urban pied-à-terre or investment asset. These buyers — many of whom have experience with urban condo markets in their home countries — are sophisticated about building financials and understand what reserve fund deficiencies signal. Currency considerations, FIRPTA withholding requirements, and foreign-national financing eligibility are standard planning elements for this buyer profile.

> **Domestic professionals** working in Brickell or the downtown core who want urban proximity without Brickell pricing. This buyer is typically financed and needs the building to meet conventional or jumbo loan requirements — which are directly affected by building financials and inspection compliance.

Each profile requires different positioning. A listing narrative that speaks to yield metrics will not resonate with a lifestyle buyer. A unit presented without building financial context will generate skepticism from an investor buyer.

## The Financing Constraint

Building financial health is not just a buyer concern — it is a lender concern. Conventional loans, FHA loans, and many jumbo loan programs have building eligibility requirements that include minimum owner-occupancy ratios, maximum delinquency thresholds, adequate insurance, and reserve funding standards.

A building that fails to meet these requirements eliminates a portion of the buyer pool immediately — specifically, financed buyers who need conforming or conventional loan eligibility. In a market where a portion of buyers are investors paying cash, this may be tolerable. In buildings where the unit type and price point primarily appeal to financed buyers, a building eligibility issue is a material problem.

Understanding the building's financing eligibility status before listing is part of the due diligence a seller should conduct, not discover.

## What to Prepare Before Listing

A Downtown Miami condo seller who wants to transact cleanly in 2026 should prepare or obtain the following before the listing goes live:

1. Building milestone inspection report (Phase 1, and Phase 2 if applicable)
2. Current association budget and most recent financial statements
3. Reserve fund balance and reserve study
4. Any pending or recently passed special assessments
5. Building's current Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac project approval status (if known)
6. HOA declaration, bylaws, rules, and any recent amendments
7. Current certificate of insurance for the building

Organizing this documentation before listing — not during the due diligence period — is the structural change that separates a smooth Downtown Miami condo transaction from one that renegotiates at inspection or falls through at financing.
